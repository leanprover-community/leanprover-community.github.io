Kevin Sullivan (Feb 26 2019 at 19:13):

As a computer scientist and software engineering guy, I've come to the conclusion that the phrase, "backwards reasoning", is not as clarifying as it could be. When we use (apply and.intro _ _), for example, sure, we can think of "going backwards through the arrow to the premises", and all that. But a better way to think of what we've done is to give a complete proof, albeit with a few remaining holes. We then recursively fill the remaining "stubs" (aka holes) by applying the same method recursively. In the software design and engineering field, this is known as top-down, structured programming. A better term might be top-down, type-guided development. The "How to Design Programs" intro CS curriculum taught at Brown, UBC, Northeastern, and other places, emphasizes exactly this type-driven, top-down approach. That said, they teach it in Racket, which doesn't have a proper type system (at least the variant of Racket that students encounter early on).

What I'm teaching my students this semester is what I humorously tell them is my "How To Design Proofs" approach. Look at the form of the goal: the top-level quantifier or connective. Select a proof-constructing function (inference rule or previously proven theorem) that can construct a proof of that form. Apply that "rule" leaving zero or more of its arguments to be filled in later, i.e., "stub out the subroutines". They then appear as sub-goals. Apply the same approach recursively to each sub-goal. If you really get stuck, maybe you picked the wrong proof-building function, so backtrack and try again. How To Design Proofs. --Kevin Sullivan
